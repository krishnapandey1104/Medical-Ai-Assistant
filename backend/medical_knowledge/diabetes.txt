Diabetes Overview

Diabetes mellitus is a chronic condition that affects how the body processes blood glucose (blood sugar). Glucose is an important source of energy for the body's cells.

Types of Diabetes

Type 1 Diabetes
Type 1 diabetes occurs when the immune system attacks and destroys insulin-producing cells in the pancreas. People with type 1 diabetes must take insulin to survive.

Type 2 Diabetes
Type 2 diabetes occurs when the body becomes resistant to insulin or when the pancreas cannot produce enough insulin. It is the most common form of diabetes.

Gestational Diabetes
Gestational diabetes develops during pregnancy and usually disappears after childbirth, but it increases the risk of developing type 2 diabetes later in life.

Symptoms of Diabetes

Common symptoms include:

Frequent urination
Increased thirst
Increased hunger
Unexplained weight loss
Fatigue
Blurred vision
Slow healing wounds
Frequent infections

Normal Blood Sugar Levels

Fasting Blood Glucose:
Normal: 70–99 mg/dL
Prediabetes: 100–125 mg/dL
Diabetes: 126 mg/dL or higher

Random Blood Sugar:
Diabetes may be diagnosed if blood sugar is 200 mg/dL or higher with symptoms.

HbA1c Test:
Normal: Below 5.7%
Prediabetes: 5.7–6.4%
Diabetes: 6.5% or higher

Risk Factors for Type 2 Diabetes

Obesity
Family history of diabetes
Physical inactivity
Poor diet
High blood pressure
High cholesterol
Age over 45

Complications

If not properly managed, diabetes can lead to serious complications:

Heart disease
Kidney damage
Nerve damage
Eye damage
Foot problems
Stroke

Prevention and Management

Healthy lifestyle changes can help manage or prevent type 2 diabetes:

Regular physical activity
Balanced diet with controlled carbohydrate intake
Maintaining a healthy body weight
Monitoring blood glucose levels
Regular medical check-ups

Treatment may include lifestyle changes, oral medications, or insulin therapy prescribed by healthcare professionals.
